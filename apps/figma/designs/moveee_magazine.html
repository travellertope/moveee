Afrobeats at the Crossroads: A Conversation with Adekunle Gold

On globalisation, authenticity, and what it means to make music that travels.

FO
Fola Olawale Culture Editor · Published June 9, 2026
12 min read

There's a particular brand of artistic confidence that Adekunle Gold carries into every room. At 34, the Yoruba pop architect has stopped explaining himself to anyone. His evolution from highlife-infused sweetheart to an unapologetic rockstar is a masterclass in sonic pivoting.

"The world wants Afrobeats but sometimes I wonder if it knows what Afrobeats actually is."

— Adekunle Gold

He's speaking to me from his Lagos studio, and I can hear the city in the background — motorcycle engines, hawkers, the faint hum of a generator. It's an auditory tapestry that anchors his latest project, even as the melodies soar towards global arenas.

Adekunle Gold photographed in Lagos, 2026.

When I ask about the label deal and the pressures of catering to a Western audience, he leans back. "I make music for me first. If it resonates in London or Los Angeles, that's a blessing. But the heartbeat? The heartbeat is always home."
