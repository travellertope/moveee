Afrobeats at the Crossroads: A Conversation with Adekunle Gold

On globalisation, authenticity, and what it means to make music that travels.

Words by Fola Olawale Jun 9, 2026 · 12 min read
Culture Economy Pt. 3

There's a particular brand of artistic confidence that Adekunle Gold carries into every room. At 34, the Yoruba pop architect has stopped explaining himself to anyone — and the music is louder for it.

He's speaking to me from his Lagos studio, and I can hear the city in the background — motorcycle engines, the distant singsong of market hawkers...

Article Top
Adekunle Gold Interview

When I ask about the international label deal that fell through in 2023, he doesn't flinch. 'They wanted to erase the Lagos in my voice. I said no. Some deals aren't worth making.'

"The world wants Afrobeats but sometimes I wonder if it knows what Afrobeats actually is."

— Adekunle Gold

The question of authenticity in a globalised music economy is not academic — it is the defining creative tension of his generation. Burna Boy wrestled with it. Tems navigated it with instinct. Gold approaches it philosophically.

Adekunle Gold photographed in Lagos, 2026.
