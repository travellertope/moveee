The Return of the Gele.

How a headwrap became a manifesto for a new generation of Nigerian women — from Lagos ateliers to Brooklyn brownstones.

The Argument By Adaeze Nwosu
Photographs by Stephen Tayo

For decades, the gele was ceremony — Sunday clothes, wedding fabric, the thing your mother tied for you before a party. Now a generation of women is wearing it differently, and meaning something different by it. This is not nostalgia. This is instruction.

Wuraola Ajetunmobi ties her first gele of the morning while her kettle is still boiling. She is thirty-four, a tax attorney, and she has been wearing a headwrap to work, in some form, every day for the past two years. She is not doing this for Instagram. She is not doing it for her mother. She is doing it, she tells me, because she got tired of explaining her head to other people.

"There was this specific moment," she says, pouring the tea. "I was in a client meeting in Victoria Island, the room was full of suits, and one of the partners kept looking at my hair like it was a question he hadn't been asked. And I thought — I can make this a question, or I can make this a statement. The gele was a statement."

Across Lagos, Abuja, Accra, and — increasingly — in London, Brooklyn, Toronto and Berlin, a generation of Nigerian women in their late twenties and thirties are doing something their mothers would recognise and their grandmothers would probably approve of: they are putting a gele on their heads on ordinary days, for ordinary reasons, and watching what happens.

Chapter ISunday clothes, undone

To understand the return, you have to understand the leaving. For most of the twentieth century, among the Yoruba, Igbo, Edo and others across southern Nigeria, the gele was a codified object. It belonged to specific days — Sundays, weddings, funerals, naming ceremonies, chieftaincy installations. It was tied by specialists, or by an aunt who knew. It was, in the broadest sense, ceremony clothes.

Then came everything else. Structural adjustment. The relative straightening of Nigerian hair under pressure from school and the office. The migration wave of the 1990s and 2000s that scattered a generation across the diaspora. A global fashion industry that briefly — between roughly 2010 and 2016 — discovered West African prints, attached them to Western silhouettes, and moved on.

↑ Fig. 01 — Balogun Market, Lagos Island. Where most of the fabric in this story begins its life. Photograph by Stephen Tayo.

Somewhere in all of that, the gele — the everyday, everyday-ish, workplace, non-ceremonial gele — quietly went away. It became a thing you owned for events. It lived in the top of your wardrobe, in a zip-lock bag, beside the shoes you only wore to weddings.

"My mother's generation wore gele every Sunday to church," says Fola Akande, a Lagos-based stylist who has been documenting the resurgence on her Substack for the past year. "My generation wore it twice a year. This generation — the one coming up now — they are wearing it on a Tuesday to the bank. That is the whole argument, right there."

"My mother wore it on Sundays. I wore it twice a year. These women are wearing it on a Tuesday to the bank. That is the whole argument."

Fola AkandeStylist, Lagos

Chapter IIAnatomy of a wrap

Before we go any further: the gele is not one thing. The word, Yoruba in origin, covers a wide family of head coverings that differ in fabric, method, meaning, and occasion. A gele is not a turban. A gele is not a headscarf. A gele is not a headwrap in the African-American sense, though it is cousin to all of them.

★ Field Guide · 01

How to read a gele

A brief visual glossary, because the shape is carrying more information than most foreign readers can see.

  • A
    The flare The tail or fan left deliberately loose. Its direction and height signal the wearer's confidence — and, historically, her marital status.
  • B
    The crown The highest point of the wrap. A higher crown means a more formal occasion; a soft, low crown reads as everyday.
  • C
    The principal fold Where the aso-oke or fabric is bent back on itself. The sharpness of this line reveals the skill of the tier.
  • D
    The base band The tight foundation around the hairline that holds the structure. In a day-wear gele, this is usually softer and lower.
  • E
    The frame How the ears, jawline and earrings are revealed or hidden. Exposing the ears is a modern, street-level move.

The distinctions matter because the new gele-wearers are choosing very specifically. Most are wearing softer fabrics — cotton, linen, adire, sometimes silk — rather than the rigid aso-oke of formal occasions. The base is lower. The flare is usually smaller. The whole composition is built to survive an actual day: a commute, a meeting, three hours at a desk, the harmattan wind.

Chapter IIIThe Brooklyn problem

Among the women I spoke to for this story, roughly half live outside Nigeria. Some have never lived there. For them, the return of the gele carries a weight that the Lagos-based wearers, talking to me over bean soup in Yaba, describe with more amusement than anxiety.

"Here in Lagos, when I tie my gele on a Tuesday, I am having a conversation with my mother and my aunt and the woman at the ATM," says Amara Onyekwere, twenty-nine, a product manager in Ikeja. "We all know what we are talking about. In Brooklyn my friend Tolu ties her gele on a Tuesday and she is having a conversation with, like, the entire history of Black women in America who were told their hair was unprofessional. She has to carry all of that."

Tolu — Tolulope Alabi, thirty-one, a public defender in Brooklyn — agrees, but with a shrug. "I know what it looks like to my co-workers. I have had to explain it more times than I can count. But I also know what it looks like to me in the mirror at six in the morning, and that is the conversation I am actually having."

Chapter IVNot a costume

There is a charge that gets levelled at any revival of traditional dress, and the gele-wearers hear it often: that they are cosplaying, that they are aestheticising heritage, that they are turning their grandmothers' clothing into branding. The women I spoke to took this charge seriously and almost all of them, in different words, rejected it.

The distinction they drew was between performance and practice. Performance is what you do once, for a camera. Practice is what you do on Tuesdays, in the dark, before anyone sees you. The gele, these women insist, is a practice. Nobody is watching when you tie it the first time in the morning. Nobody is watching when you fix it at lunch. The wrap happens whether or not anyone validates it.

"My mother tied my first gele for me when I was five," says Ajetunmobi. "She was teaching me something. When I tie one now, I am finishing what she started. It is not because of her and it is not for her. It is the thing itself. She happened to be the first person to show it to me."

Chapter VWhat we mean by return

The word return implies a thing that once left. But the gele never fully left. It stayed in Sunday clothes, in wedding albums, in the top shelves of wardrobes, in the muscle memory of the women who still tied each other's wraps before parties. What returned, rather, was the weekday version — the casual gele, the undramatic gele, the gele that does not announce.

This is what a living tradition looks like. A thing that stays close enough to be picked up, put down, picked up again. The women who are tying their wraps on ordinary mornings are not restoring something lost. They are choosing, deliberately, to keep something alive.

On my last day reporting, I meet Ajetunmobi again at a café in Lekki. She has come straight from court. The gele is indigo today, softer than the one she tied at breakfast. She sees me looking at it and smiles.

"I tie a different one after lunch," she says, as if this were the most ordinary thing in the world. And for her, increasingly, it is.
