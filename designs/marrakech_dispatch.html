Three Nights at the
Marrakech
Film Festival.

An outdoor cinema in Jemaa el-Fna, a Senegalese retrospective that stopped the room, and the strange feeling of watching African cinema where African cinema belongs.

The thing nobody tells you about the Marrakech International Film Festival is that the best screening I attended was not in a cinema. It was in Jemaa el-Fna, the great square at the heart of the medina, under a sky that had spent the evening turning through five shades of apricot before settling, finally, on black. The screen was enormous. The chairs were plastic. The film was Djibril Diop Mambéty's Touki Bouki, projected onto a temporary scrim between two palm trees, and when the credits rolled three hundred people sat in silence for what felt like a very long time.

I had come to Marrakech expecting the festival's reputation — prestigious, star-heavy, a little removed from the African cinema it nominally champions. I had heard the complaints: that the red carpet arrivals dominate the coverage, that the jury prizes go where prizes always go, that the local audience is an afterthought. Some of this is true. Some of it is unfair. All of it is more complicated than it sounds.

Night OneThe square & the retrospective

I arrived on a Thursday. The city was already in festival mode — the riad owners had draped their terraces in red and gold, the taxis were playing Oumou Sangaré instead of the usual French pop, and the passport queue at Menara Airport contained at least one person I was fairly sure was a famous French actor, though I couldn't place him without Google. I was collected by a young man named Youssef who told me, unprompted, that his favourite film was No Country for Old Men and that he had seen it eleven times. I told him about the Mambéty screening. He had never heard of Mambéty. By the time we reached the riad, he had looked him up and was watching clips of Hyènes on his phone while stationary at a red light, which felt both dangerous and appropriate.

↑ Fig. 01 — Open-air screening, Jemaa el-Fna square. The Mambéty retrospective ran four consecutive nights.

The retrospective was the festival's centrepiece, and the only part of the programme that felt genuinely necessary rather than merely well-organised. Four films over four nights: Badou Boy, Touki Bouki, Hyènes, and La Petite Vendeuse de Soleil — the latter technically by Clarence Delgado, completing Mambéty's unfinished Tales of Ordinary People cycle. Each film introduced by a different critic or filmmaker, each introduction a small argument for why Mambéty's restless, non-linear way of seeing the world matters now as much as it mattered when Touki Bouki premiered at Cannes in 1973 and promptly lost the Palme d'Or to a film nobody discusses any more.

★ Film of the Night

Touki Bouki

Dir. Djibril Diop Mambéty · Senegal · 1973 · 85 min

A road movie, a love story, a manifesto, and a long argument with postcolonial Dakar — all at once. The film Mambéty made at 27 that the world called experimental and Africa called true. Watching it in Jemaa el-Fna, with the Atlas mountains somewhere in the dark behind you, is an experience that cannot be replicated by a streaming service.

★★★★★

Night TwoThe competition & the conversation

The main competition films were screened in the Palais des Congrès, a venue that has the particular quality of all large official cultural spaces — functional, slightly cold, designed to process large numbers of people rather than to move them. The films were a mixed bag, as competition films always are. Three from the African continent, four from elsewhere. A prestige European production about World War I that the jury would eventually reward with something it didn't quite deserve. A debut from a Cameroonian director named Awa Nkemdirim whose second feature will, I am confident, be the one everyone talks about in five years.

Between screenings, the festival does what festivals do: it organises panels that are attended by the wrong people, meals that the right people attend without invitation, and parties where the two groups occasionally, productively collide. I spent the better part of Night Two's daytime hours at a roundtable on African film distribution that was, improbably, the most energising two hours of the week.

"The problem is not that African cinema doesn't exist. The problem is that the infrastructure that should carry it to its audience has been built by people who don't believe the audience exists."
— Awa Nkemdirim, speaking at the Marrakech distribution roundtable

Nkemdirim was the youngest person in the room and the one who seemed most impatient with the framing of the conversation — that African cinema needs to "reach" audiences, as if those audiences were a destination the films had yet to discover. "The audience is there," she said. "It has always been there. The question is who controls the road between them and the screen." The room was very quiet for a moment after that. Then a French distributor said something about rights windows and the moment passed.

Night ThreeThe closing night & what stays with you

The closing ceremony is the part of every film festival that everyone attends and nobody particularly enjoys. The speeches are long. The prizes go partly where they should and partly where the political logic of the jury demands. The after-party is excellent. I mention all of this not to be cynical — the ceremony is genuinely moving in the way that all public acts of collective attention are moving — but to say that it was not the high point of Night Three.

The high point was earlier. At six in the evening, in a small screening room on the second floor of a riad that had been converted for the festival's fringe programme, a Ghanaian filmmaker named Emmanuel Apau showed a forty-minute documentary about the spare parts markets of Accra. No distributor. No press. Eleven people in the room, including me, including the filmmaker's mother, who had flown from Kumasi for the occasion and who wept quietly throughout and then stood up at the end and said something in Twi that Apau translated, approximately, as: "She says you captured it exactly and she wants to know if you need dinner."

This is why you go to festivals. Not for the red carpet, not for the official competition, not even for the Mambéty retrospective — though that was extraordinary. You go for the forty-minute documentary in the second-floor room. You go for the filmmaker's mother. You go for the moment when a story finds its audience and the room understands, before anyone says a word, that something true has just happened.

I flew home on a Sunday morning. In the taxi to Menara, Youssef told me he had watched all four Mambéty films on YouTube. He said Hyènes was his new favourite film of all time, displacing No Country for Old Men, which he now felt he would need to reconsider. I told him that seemed about right. We drove through the palmery as the sun came up behind the Atlas, and the city looked, as it sometimes does at that hour, like a thing that has been dreaming.
