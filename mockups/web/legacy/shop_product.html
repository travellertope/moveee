The Object

A scarf is a piece of fabric. This scarf is the end of a fourteen-day conversation with a leaf.

Field notes · Abeokuta

Adunni Adewunmi has been working with indigo for twenty-three years. She learned it from her mother, who learned it from her grandmother, who learned it — Adunni says, with a slight shrug that suggests the matter is both very precise and slightly unknowable — "from somewhere before that." Her studio sits on a quiet plot outside Abeokuta, an hour north of Lagos, and most mornings she is up before the call to prayer to check the dye vats.

The vats are the whole business. Adire, in the strictest Yoruba sense, is a resist-dye technique — you bind, stitch, or paint paste onto cloth to protect parts of it from the dye, then immerse the fabric in indigo and watch the pattern emerge when you untie it. The word adire can refer to the technique, the tradition, or the cloth itself. This scarf, Adunni tells me, took fourteen days.

"A good indigo takes its time. If the cloth is in a hurry, the colour can tell." Adunni AdewunmiFounder, Ile Oluji Studio

What you are holding, when you hold this scarf, is the result of a slow, patient, occasionally frustrating negotiation between a woman, a plant, and a piece of silk. The silk was sourced from a mill in Aso, Nigeria. The indigo leaves were harvested from Adunni's own plot, dried, pounded, and fermented in clay pots for six days before the dyeing began. The pattern — concentric circles and diamonds, in the oniko style — was hand-tied by Adunni and two of her nieces over the course of a morning.

Then the scarf went into the dye. Out of the dye. Aired in the shade. Back into the dye. Aired again. Ten times, over eleven days. On the twelfth day, Adunni untied the bindings. On the thirteenth, the scarf dried. On the fourteenth, it was steamed, pressed, and wrapped.

Field Guide · 02

How it's made

A fourteen-day conversation between silk, leaf, and the patience of one woman in Abeokuta.

01

Tie the resist

Raffia and cotton thread are used to bind the silk into small, precise knots. Wherever the fabric is tied, the dye cannot reach. This is where the pattern lives.

Day 1 · 6 hours
02

Ferment the indigo

Dried and pounded indigo leaves are mixed with ash water and left to ferment in clay pots for six days. The vat is alive — it must be checked morning and evening.

Days 2–7 · 6 days
03

Dip, air, repeat

The cloth is submerged in the vat, then pulled and aired — the real colour appears only on contact with oxygen. Ten cycles over eleven days build the depth.

Days 8–11 · 10 dips
04

Untie, dry, press

Bindings are removed by hand. The pattern is revealed only now — never seen during the work. The scarf is steamed, pressed, and wrapped for shipping.

Days 12–14 · Finish

Ile Oluji Studio

Founded in 2012 by Adunni Adewunmi on a small plot outside Abeokuta, Ile Oluji Studio is a five-woman workshop specialising in hand-fermented indigo and traditional adire techniques. The studio grows its own indigo, trains apprentices from the surrounding village, and produces no more than sixty pieces a month — fewer during the rainy season, when the vats behave differently.
