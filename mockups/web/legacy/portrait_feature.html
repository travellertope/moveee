The Quiet Insurgency of Ifeoma Okoli.

She paints in the back room of a Yaba bungalow, rarely gives interviews, and refuses to ship her work outside Nigeria. In a market obsessed with the global, Ifeoma Okoli is building something stubbornly local — and the rest of the world is starting to take notice.

The bungalow is unmarked. No sign, no number — Ifeoma Okoli likes it this way, and so do her neighbours. You arrive by the name of a kiosk at the junction, turn left at the tailor, and walk until you see a metal gate the colour of old blood. Behind it: a yard, a mango tree, two cats, and the room where she has made nearly everything she has ever made.

It is a Wednesday in March when I visit. The harmattan has finally lifted and the air is loose again. Okoli, thirty-one, meets me at the gate in a paint-stained smock and leads me past the cats without introducing them. "They hate guests," she says, not unkindly. "Don't take it personally."

Inside, the back room is smaller than I expected. Four canvases lean against one wall — the last four pieces of her forthcoming show at Kó Gallery, titled Mother Tongue — and a fifth sits on the easel, half-finished. A woman's face, rendered in the quiet, flattened style that has become her signature, stares out from layers of ochre and indigo. The eyes are closed. The mouth is parted, as if mid-word.

Chapter IA back room in Yaba

Okoli did not come up through the usual routes. She did not attend Yabatech or the Ahmadu Bello University art school. She did not intern with any of the Lagos art-world mothers who now quietly champion her. She studied accounting at the University of Nigeria, Nsukka, and for four years after graduation she worked in the back office of a midsize bank in Enugu, painting on the weekends and — for a while — not really painting at all.

"I thought I had given it up," she says. "I had the job, the flat, the plan. Then my mother died and I couldn't do the numbers any more. Literally. I would sit at the desk and the columns would swim."

↑ Fig. 02 — Okoli's studio, March 2026. An unfinished work from Mother Tongue on the easel.

She moved to Lagos in 2022 with almost nothing. A friend of a friend rented her the back room for the price of the electricity bill. She found work teaching primary school art part-time and painted the rest of the hours she had. The first series — fourteen small oil portraits of women she half-remembered from her mother's funeral — sold out in a single afternoon at a pop-up in Surulere, to buyers she had never heard of.

It was, she says, the first time she understood that painting could be a job. It was also, she says, the last time she sold a work quickly.

Chapter IIRefusing the market

By 2024 the offers had started. A London dealer flew in with a contract. A New York gallery wanted eight pieces for a group show. An advisor called from Dubai about a private commission in the high six figures. Okoli turned them all down. She still turns them all down.

"I don't want my work in a warehouse in Geneva," she tells me, pouring tea from a chipped flask. "I don't want it on a wall I will never see, owned by a person whose name I will never learn. That is not a philosophical position. It is a practical one. The paintings are about specific people. If the people who know those people can't see the work, the work is doing nothing."

"If the people who know those people can't see the work, the work is doing nothing."

— Ifeoma Okoli

This is the line that travels. It has been quoted in half a dozen publications over the past eighteen months, sometimes approvingly, sometimes as the setup for a gentle diagnosis — that Okoli is romanticising a local art economy that does not yet exist; that refusing the global market is a luxury her collectors are subsidising; that one cannot, in the end, feed a practice on principle alone.

She has heard all of this. She is not persuaded. Her paintings are priced in naira, not dollars, and the waiting list for a new Okoli is now, by her dealer's estimate, a little over two years long. Every buyer is vetted. Most are Nigerian. A few are from the diaspora, and those she considers carefully. One piece — Portrait of a Woman I Do Not Remember — hangs in the living room of a retired nurse in Ikoyi who bought it, in instalments, over eleven months.

Chapter IIIOn being seen

We talk, eventually, about visibility. It is a word she dislikes — too thin, she says, for what it's being asked to carry. She prefers recognition, which to her suggests mutual obligation: you see me, I see you, we are held by the seeing.

This, more than anything, is the argument of Mother Tongue. The show is made up of twenty-three portraits of women who speak Igbo as a first language and who, for one reason or another, have spent most of their adult lives somewhere else. Okoli found them through her mother's church network, through cousins, through a single Facebook post that circulated for one week in 2024 and was then deleted. She visited each sitter at least twice. She painted them from memory. None of them have seen their portraits yet.

"They will see them at the opening," she says. "At the same time as everybody else. That is the point. Recognition is a public act. It has to be witnessed."

Chapter IVWhat comes next

The light in the back room is starting to go. Okoli walks me past the canvases one more time, naming each sitter quietly as we pass — not for me, I realise, but for herself, a small ritual of acknowledgement. At the gate the cats are waiting. They still hate me.

I ask what she is working on after Mother Tongue. She pauses longer than I expect. "A series about the women who raised other people's children," she says finally. "My mother was one of them, before she had me. It is going to take years. I am in no hurry."

Outside, the light is orange and the mango tree is a dark shape against it, and somewhere behind me Ifeoma Okoli has already closed the gate and gone back to work.
